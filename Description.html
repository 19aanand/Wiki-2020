The Problem


For a number of decades, the agricultural industry has sought to enact the benefits of genetically modified (GM) crops to improve crop yields and mitigate crop losses due to pests and pathogens. GM crops can decrease pesticide usage in large-scale agricultural production[1] and uptake nutrients more efficiently than their unmodified counterparts[2].

While genetically modified organisms (GMOs) have revolutionized the current worldview of agriculture, farmers and scientists alike have raised concerns over their negative effects on the environment. Particularly, gene flow—the transfer of genetic variation from one population to another—from cultivated crops to weedy or wild crops is a potential risk of commercializing GM crops.

Gene flow can decrease genetic variation within a population by modifying allele frequencies[3]. If the rate of gene flow is high enough, GM crops can outcompete their wild-type (WT) counterparts, leading to decreased biodiversity.



Biodiversity's Importance


“Agricultural biodiversity,” or agrobiodiversity, consists of animals, plants, and microorganisms at the genetic, species, and ecosystem levels are necessary to sustain key functions in the agro-system[4]. Generally, greater biodiversity enables agricultural productivity over a wider range of conditions[5]. In light of climate change, maintaining agrobiodiversity is necessary to ensure the resilience of food crop production.

The 1800s Irish potato famine highlights the importance of agrobiodiversity[6]. The lack of genetic variability in the potatoes proved detrimental when the pathogen, P. infestans, wiped out the entire population. This lack of genetic variability is seen today; 7,000 plant species are used for human consumption, yet four crops provide half of the global plant-based energy intake and 15 crops contribute another two-thirds[5].

As the popularity of GMOs increases, concerns that they could reduce the biodiversity of the agricultural landscape and surrounding ecosystems also increase. The effectiveness of herbicide-resistant crops have eliminated milkweeds from many fields. However, the loss of milkweed harms insects like the monarch butterfly, which has experienced a population decline of about 80% in the last two decades[7]. Therefore, our project seeks an innovative solution to the consequences of GM crops while still reaping their benefits.



Indoor Farming - An Incomplete Solution


There are many prospective solutions to combat gene flow and its impact on agriculture. One is vertical farming, which involves cultivating produce in vertically arranged layers. It has paved the way for sustainable agricultural practices, allowing farmers and agricultural industries greater control over factors like soil and water use, and gas emission levels[8].

With hydroponic farming methods, farmers can introduce oxygen and other nutrients to plant roots, allowing for more growth cycles in less time. Traditional farming methods rely on nitrogen-containing fertilizers, which increase the amount of atmospheric nitrous oxide, a potent greenhouse gas. Growing produce in an indoor environment gives farmers more control over their crops and prevents intervention from animals, pathogens, and even other plants—reducing gene flow.

Indoor vertical farming is an effective strategy for promoting conservation and minimizing gene flow that could occur in conventional farming methods. However, this strategy lacks control at the level of an individual organism if it were to be inadvertently introduced to the environment.



Hypersensitive Response


Programmed cell death (PCD) is a common process that occurs in many organisms during development in response to environmental or biotic stimuli. One of the best characterized forms of PCD in animals is called apoptosis. However, plant cell death is not characterized as apoptosis, because plant cell death mechanisms lack some of its hallmarks. Namely, lack of caspase proteins, a major player in a cell’s commitment to apoptosis, and the inability to participate in phagocytosis due to a rigid cell wall is why controlled processes of plant cell death are referred to as PCD rather than apoptosis[9].

Instead, plants participate in a different pathway of programmed cell death known as the hypersensitive response (HR). It should be noted that, much like apoptotic mechanisms are not identical across all animals, HR in plants is also not exact across the board. Though it is not nearly as well characterized, the hallmarks of HR are strikingly similar to those found in animal apoptosis. Comparable features include cytoplasmic shrinkage, cytochrome c release, mitochondrial swelling, chromatin condensation, and loss of mitochondrial membrane potential. Additionally, plant cells undergo formation of large vesicles, disruption of the cytoskeleton and the cessation of cytoplasmic streaming during HR[10].



Bax and the Hypersensitive Response


Bax is a member of the Bcl-2 protein family. Among other processes, these proteins regulate pathways associated with apoptosis. Bcl-2 proteins can either be pro-apoptotic and work to induce apoptosis (like Bax/Bak), or anti-apoptotic and work to inhibit apoptosis (like Bcl-2 proteins). Bax is crucial for the assembly of a structure called the mitochondrial apoptosis-induced channel (MAC)[13]. This structure is a channel in the outer membrane of the mitochondria (OMM) that releases proteins that reside in the intermembrane space. In response to apoptotic-inducing insults, Bax, the pro-apoptotic protein, translocates from the cytosol to the OMM.

Once associated with the OMM, Bax oligomerizes and forms the mitochondrial apoptosis-induced channel (MAC). This protein interacts with and increases the opening of the mitochondrial voltage-gated anion channel (VDAC), which leads to loss in membrane potential. This subsequently releases cytochrome c, which triggers the permeabilization of OMM via the release of Smac1, and ultimately the activation of a caspase cascade, a key event in committing the cell to undergoing apoptosis through intrinsic pathways in animal cells[14].

Although not endogenous to plants, Bax can be expressed (crucially, overexpressed) in plant tissues, ultimately resulting in HR-like cell death. It has been suggested that this is due to the integration of Bax into the mitochondrial membrane, however, Bax expression associated with HR is not well characterized in plants.
